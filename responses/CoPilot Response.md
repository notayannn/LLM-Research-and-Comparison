The study tested whether a peer-driven, AI-answer error-correction strategy improves students' ability to apply knowledge and their metacognitive awareness compared with AI self-learning plus teacher summarization. It found the intervention group scored significantly higher on both outcomes.

Both groups completed identical 80-minute sessions (50 minutes AI-supported inquiry learning followed by 30 minutes of either teacher summarization or peer-driven error correction). In the experimental condition, small groups input teacher-recommended and self-generated questions/statements into AI chatbots, identified factual errors or omissions in AI answers, corrected them using textbooks and reliable references, and then shared corrections and verification strategies with the class.

"The AI-answer error-correction strategy improved short-term knowledge application and metacognitive awareness, offering a feasible approach to promote critical AI engagement in competency-based education." "The intervention group scored significantly higher on knowledge application (87.57±6.50 vs. 78.22±7.46, p < .001) and metacognitive awareness (4.14±0.58 vs. 3.78±0.44, p < .001)."

- **Knowledge application:** Intervention post-test adjusted mean ≈ **87.6** vs control **78.2**, mean difference **9.43**, **Cohen's d = 1.35** (p < .001).
    
- **Overall metacognition:** Intervention mean **4.14** vs control **3.78**, **Cohen's d = 0.67** (p < .001).
    
- **Metacognitive subscales:** Intervention exceeded control on **knowledge (d = 0.667)**, **monitoring (d = 0.760)**, and **regulation (d = 0.473)**, with monitoring showing the largest effect.
    

Authors recommend integrating AI as a fallible learning partner and embedding structured AI-error correction activities to promote critical evaluation and metacognitive development in higher education.
