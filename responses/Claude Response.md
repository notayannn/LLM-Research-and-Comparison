**Core exploration:** This quasi-experimental study investigated whether repositioning AI as a fallible "learning partner" to be critiqued, rather than an authoritative source, could improve medical students' knowledge application and metacognitive awareness. It specifically compared a peer-driven AI-answer error-correction strategy against a control condition of AI self-learning followed by teacher summarization, in the context of biomedical materials/sterilization education.

**Research design:** Two parallel classes of second-year clinical medicine students were assigned to a control group (n=67 after exclusions) or an intervention group (n=94 after exclusions) in a pre-test/post-test design. The error-correction phase required student groups to input teacher-recommended and self-generated questions into AI chatbots, record the AI's answers, identify factual errors or omissions, correct them using textbooks and reliable references, and then share their findings and corrections with the class.

**Impacts on knowledge application and metacognition:**

- The intervention group scored significantly higher on the post-test knowledge application assessment (87.57±6.50 vs. 78.22±7.46, p<.001), with a large effect size (Cohen's d=1.35).
- The intervention group demonstrated significantly higher overall metacognitive awareness (4.14±0.58 vs. 3.78±0.44, p<.001), with gains observed across all three subscales—knowledge, monitoring, and regulation—though monitoring showed the largest effect size (Cohen's d=0.760).
- Secondary outcomes also favored the intervention group, including more positive AI learning attitudes (4.15±0.61 vs. 3.67±0.37, p<.001) and more frequent self-reported information-verification behaviors.

**Final takeaway:** The authors recommend that AI be deliberately integrated into curricula through structured, scaffolded activities—such as peer correction of AI-generated errors—rather than simply granting unstructured access to AI tools, since intentional error-based engagement fosters the critical evaluation skills needed to counter "AI authority bias" in competency-based education.
